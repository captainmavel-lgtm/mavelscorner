Starting over has a particular kind of weight to it. It does not just feel like a loss but an evidence. Evidence that you made the wrong choices, that you missed something, that the people who doubted you were right all along. So instead of stepping into what is next, you stay stuck at the edge of it, paralysed by a story you have told yourself about what starting over means. But what if I tell you that story is wrong?

The idea that starting over is a sign of failure is one of the most damaging lies a person can carry in their mind and into their future. It may sound reasonable on the surface. After all, if things had gone right the first time, you would not need to begin all over again. But that logic collapses the moment you hold it up against Scripture, against the lives of the people God used most, and against the very nature of how God operates.

God has never been afraid of a fresh start. In fact, throughout the Bible, some of the most significant moments in human history began with someone being asked to leave behind what was familiar and step into something entirely new.

> *"Do not remember the former things, nor consider the things of old. Behold, I will do a new thing, now it shall spring forth; shall you not know it? I will even make a road in the wilderness and rivers in the desert."  Isaiah 43:18-19 (NKJV)*

God spoke these words to a people who were stuck in their past, who kept measuring their present circumstances against what they had experienced before, and His response was not to minimise what they had been through. It was to redirect their attention entirely. He was not asking them to pretend the old season did not happen. He was telling them that something new was already beginning, and they were going to miss it if they kept looking backwards.
Starting over is not God abandoning His plan for your life, but often God advancing it.

**What Starting Over Is Not**

Before you can embrace what starting over actually is, you need to dismantle what it is not.
It is not proof that you wasted your time. Every season you have lived through, including the ones that ended in ways you did not choose, shaped something in you. The skills you built, the lessons you learned, the character that was formed under pressure, none of that disappears when a chapter closes. God is never wasteful, He recycles everything for a purpose.

It is not a sign that God left. A change of direction is not the same as divine abandonment. Sometimes, God closes a door not because He is done with you but because He is protecting you from settling for something smaller than what He has prepared. The closed door is not rejection, it is redirection. And it is not a step backwards either. Progress does not always look linear. Sometimes the path forward requires you to step sideways, or even to pause entirely, before the real momentum builds. What looks like regression from the outside may be exactly the kind of repositioning God needs to do before He launches you forward.

> *"Through the Lord's mercies we are not consumed, because His compassions fail not. They are new every morning; great is Your faithfulness."  Lamentations 3:22-23 (NKJV)*

This scripture says it all. His mercies are new every morning, not new once. Not new when you have earned it. New every single morning, without any condition, without any qualification. The mercy of God does not carry yesterday's disappointments into today. Every morning is a clean slate, a fresh invitation to begin again under the covering of a God whose faithfulness does not fluctuate with your circumstances.

Starting over is not a crisis in God's economy. It is built into the rhythm of how He operates. Mercy renewed daily is God's way of telling you that new beginnings are not the exception in His Kingdom, but the norm.

> *"Forgetting those things which are behind and reaching forward to those things which are ahead, I press toward the goal for the prize of the upward call of God in Christ Jesus."  Philippians 3:13-14 (NKJV)*

Paul wrote these words as a man who had everything the world considered impressive and chose to release all of it. He did not drift into starting over. He made a deliberate decision to forget what was behind and press toward what was ahead. Starting over, for Paul, was not a defeat. It was an act of faith. It was him saying that what God was calling him toward was worth more than what he was leaving behind.

God has not given up on you. The new beginning He is placing in front of you is not a consolation prize for a life that did not work out. It is a calling; it is His hand, reaching into the rubble of what used to be, and building something you could not have imagined from where you were standing before.

But if starting over is truly a calling and not a failure, what does it actually look like to answer that call? That is exactly what we are going to explore in Part 2.

## Something To Think About.

What is the story you have been telling yourself about what your starting over means, and where did that story actually come from?
Is there a door God may have closed in your life that you are still trying to reopen, when He may be waiting for you to turn and see the new one?
What would change about how you see your current season if you genuinely believed that starting over was God's idea and not your failure?

## Prayer.

Lord, I have carried so much shame about where I am starting from, and I am tired of the weight of it. I choose today to believe that You are not finished with me and that this new beginning is not a punishment but a gift. Help me to release what is behind me without bitterness and to reach toward what is ahead without fear. I trust that Your mercies are new today, and that is enough to take the next step. Lead me forward Lord in Jesus’ mighty name, Amen.

## Challenge.

Then, underneath it, write this sentence: *“God is building something new here, and I choose to make room for it.”* Carry that sentence with you through the week and let it begin to replace the narrative that starting over means you failed.
